# No Gyro

Let's start by assuming the player doesn't want gyro.

There are two ways the player can move the camera.

## Reset Camera

Press the Y button to reset the camera behind the player.

## This is NOT calibration

> Contrary to many beliefs, this does __NOT__ calibrate the gyro.
>Gyro calibration happens automatically in game at all times.
>To see proper gyro calibration, have an opportunity to place the controller down flat on the desk (like when you get splatted), and pull up the map.
>You may see the circular cursor move on its own even while not moving the controller.
>But if you **don't** move the controller for no more than three seconds, the cursor will stop moving on its own.
>The gyro is now calibrated. It happened automatically.

## What Y Button actually does

Using the Y button does two things, regardless if gyro is enabled.

1. The camera pivots behind the player, facing the direction they are facing or moving.
2. The camera also rebalances its pitch *almost* back to level.

What 1. means is: any player can always have the camera face their intended direction, and this is much faster and more reliable than either stick or gyro movement.

What 2. means is that the Recenter Camera helps but not always angles the camera back to center; it still depends on how away the player looked up or down.

- If the player was looking at the floor, resetting the camera will rebalance it a little below level.
- If the player was looking at the sky, resetting the camera will rebalance it a little above level.

## Hold the button

In addition, holding Y and moving the character will cause them to strafe while still looking in the direction of the cursor.

Release the button, and they won't be fixated on the cursor anymore; the character normally faces the direction they are moving until they fire or throw their main or sub weapon, respectively.

> This is more useful for showing opponents where you're looking, not so much for you, the player to see where the character is looking.

## Right Stick

Push the right analog stick left to spin the camera to face the left.

Push the right analog stick right to spin the camera to face the right.

Push the right analog stick up to tilt the camera to face up.

Push the right analog stick down to tilt the camera to face down.

> Note that you cannot tilt the camera up or down too far.
# Gyro On

Now, let's enable the gyro and see the other ways a player can use the camera.

## Understand Axes

Splatoon responds to the changes in your controller's movements, based on three distinct axes.
## Yaw

> Imagine a bus steering wheel.

Keep the controller flat on a table, buttons facing up and USB-C port facing the TV screen.

> Imagine a laser shooting out of the USB-C port on the top of the controller.

Rotate the controller to pivot the laser and point to the left side of the TV screen, then the right.

This movement rotates the horizontal camera, __as if you're moving the Right analog stick left or right__.

## Roll

> Imagine a seesaw.

Orient the controller upright. The buttons face you, the player, while the USB-C port points to the sky.

> Imagine the laser shooting out of the USB-C port on the top of the controller is pointing at the sky.

Pull one handle of the controller towards you, and push the other handle away. Vice versa.

This movement rotates the horizontal camera, __as if you're moving the Right analog stick left or right__.

## Yaw vs. Roll

Note that we have two more ways to move the camera horizontally, but their movement depends on how the controller is held.

### Try one movement in the other position.

#### Roll while flat

Orient the controller flat on the desk, buttons facing up and port pointing to the TV screen.

Pull up on one handle and push the other.

Notice the camera doesn't move much?

#### Yaw while upright

Orient the controler upright. The buttons face you, the player, while the port points to the sky.

Spin the controller around like a car steering wheel.

Notice the camera doesn't move much?
## The Truth

> A player can always rotate the controller on their yaw or roll, or both. It all depends on their pitch angle, which can change at any time in game.

## Pitch

> Imagine the controller as an airplane. The top side where the charging port is the front; the handles are the wings.

Lay the controller flat on the table, buttons facing up and the charging port on top.

> Imagine a laser shooting out of the top of the controller, pointing at the TV screen.

Tilt back the controller, moving the laser to the top of the screen. Tilt it forward, moving the laser to the button.

This movement rotates the vertical camera, __as if you're moving the Right analog stick up or down__.

## Use all three axes.

Tilt the controller at a 45-degree angle, pointing slightly above the top of the TV screen.

Try a yaw rotation, then a roll rotation, then the pitch.

All three work to move the camera, as long as you're around this angle.

And the amount one of the horizontal axes can contribute to the camera depends on the pitch angle.

- Tilt higher, and the roll contributes more.
- Tilt lower, and the yaw contributes more.

### The third way to use gyro to look around

Orient the controller sideways; the buttons face the left of you, the player, and the charging port laser is still pointing to the TV screen.

Pivot the controller on the same axis as before; tilt it back up, then forward down.

This movement rotates the horizontal camera, __as if you're moving the Right analog stick left or right__.
## But I don't want the other axis

__Are you sure?__

## The harsh truth about camera movement

Do __NOT__ treat horizontal and vertical camera movement as the same.

Vertical camera movement is clamped to a specific range of angles. Otherwise, the player can break their spin to look behind them upside down.

On the other hand, the horizontal camera movement can spin around the player forever. There's no clamping.

Therefore, __you need more ways to spin the camera around than you need to point up or down__.

> Rather than stick to a preference, understand these tools you have to master camera movement.
